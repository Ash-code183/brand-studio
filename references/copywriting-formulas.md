# Landing Page Copywriting Formulas

## Hero Headline Formulas

Never: "We help X do Y" — passive, forgettable
Never: "The platform for X" — generic, no transformation
Never: "Introducing [Product]" — feature-first, not benefit-first

### Formula 1: The Transformation Statement
**[FROM STATE] → [TO STATE]**
Example: "Stop managing spreadsheets. Start closing deals."

### Formula 2: The Bold Claim
**[Superlative outcome] for [specific person]**
Example: "The fastest way to hire engineers who can actually build."

### Formula 3: The Provocative Question
**[Pain question]** (answer is your product)
Example: "Why does shipping a feature still take three weeks?"

### Formula 4: The Specific Outcome
**[Specific result] in [specific time] without [common frustration]**
Example: "Rank on page one in 90 days — without writing a single word yourself."

### Formula 5: The Category Creation
**[New category name]: [brief definition]**
Example: "Agentic AI for operations teams: software that works, not just answers."

---

## Subheadline Formulas

The subheadline answers: "Who is this for and what specific pain does it solve?"

Template: "[Product] gives [specific customer] [specific outcome] without [common frustration]."
Example: "Harvest gives freelancers accurate project profitability without manual time logging."

Or: "Built for [specific person] who [specific situation]."
Example: "Built for founders who need to move fast without breaking things."

---

## CTA Copy Rules

**Primary CTA** (high commitment): Action verb + outcome
- "Start building for free"
- "Get your brand kit"
- "See it live"
- NOT: "Learn more", "Get started", "Submit"

**Secondary CTA** (low commitment): Same action, lower commitment
- "See a demo" (vs "Buy now")
- "Read the docs" (vs "Sign up")
- "Watch 2-min video" (vs "Book a call")

---

## Benefit Headline Formula

Never describe what the feature IS — describe what it DOES for the user.

**[User action] [outcome they care about]**
Example: "Ship 10x faster without breaking prod" (not "Advanced CI/CD pipeline")

Or: **[Problem solved] + [gain achieved]**
Example: "No more context switching. One place for everything." (not "Unified dashboard")

---

## Social Proof Formulas

### Numbers (make them specific, not round)
- "Used by 3,400+ teams" > "Used by thousands of teams"
- "97% of users ship faster in week 1" > "Users love it"
- "4.9/5 from 847 reviews" > "Highly rated"

### Names (specific > generic)
- "Teams at Notion, Stripe, and Linear use [product]" > "Enterprise companies"
- "Backed by founders who built [known company]" > "Experienced team"

### Outcomes (specific transformation)
- "Cut deployment time from 4 hours to 12 minutes"
- "Reduced churn by 23% in 60 days"

## FAQ Anti-Patterns

**Bad FAQ**: "What is [Product]?" — This is a failure. If someone is reading the FAQ, they already know what it is.

**Good FAQ questions**: The questions your most skeptical prospect has 5 minutes before they leave the page.
1. "How long does it take to get started?" (implementation anxiety)
2. "What does it cost? Are there hidden fees?" (price anxiety)
3. "Is this right for my company size/stage?" (fit anxiety)
4. "What happens if I want to cancel?" (lock-in anxiety)

---

## The Inverted Pyramid Structure

Every landing page tells one story in order of descending importance:

1. **What** — What you do (hero headline)
2. **For whom** — Exact customer (subheadline)
3. **Why you** — Your unfair advantage (benefits)
4. **How** — How it works (steps)
5. **Proof** — Testimonials + logos
6. **Answer objections** — FAQ
7. **Call to action** — Repeat CTA with urgency

Never let the page get stuck on features. Every sentence earns its place by answering "so what?"

## Voice Calibration by Category

**B2B / Enterprise SaaS**
- Tone: Confident, precise, outcome-obsessed
- Avoid: Exclamation points, casual slang, emojis in headlines
- Lead with: ROI, time savings, risk reduction

**Developer Tools**
- Tone: Peer-to-peer, technically credible, respects intelligence
- Avoid: Marketing speak, vague claims, oversimplified analogies
- Lead with: What you can build, performance specs, integration depth

**Consumer Apps**
- Tone: Warm, direct, conversational
- Avoid: Corporate speak, acronyms, passive voice
- Lead with: Emotional outcome, daily life improvement, delight

**Fintech / Regulated Industries**
- Tone: Trustworthy, specific, reassuring
- Avoid: Hype, "revolutionary", "disrupting"
- Lead with: Security, compliance, control, track record

**Creator / Indie Hacker**
- Tone: Fellow builder, anti-corporate, honest about limitations
- Avoid: Hollow claims, VC-speak, polished perfection
- Lead with: Real numbers, the maker's story, unfair transparency
